Session 4 — The Con(stable) Job

Year 873 · Simswald · Days 25–29 Wet on the 26th, warm and clear by the 29th

Jack and Henrick woke on the twenty-fifth of Simswald three short of a party. They had learned, in the days before, that the Hlerribuck breggle festival was drawing near — a celebration with a public face open to the town and a private gathering for breggles alone at Shadhome, the seat of breggle power in the region. For now, none of that was their concern. They were looking for Lucien.

Margery Stallowmead, wiping down the bar at Hornstoat's Rest, confirmed what little she knew: the elf had bought himself some expensive drinks and set off into the woods. He had not been with the three breggles. He had been alone. Jack pressed a few coins into the hand of a poor child named Jeremy with a simple commission — keep watch on the Constable's comings and goings — and then the two of them set out for the edge of the wood, following the pull of the Nuncle Stones.

They found something near the tree line: a small handmade pin shaped like a vial. It nagged at Jack. He thought of an apothecary he'd caught wind of — a place called the Man of Gold — and followed the thread. The shop crouched in a narrow alley near Hornstoat's squat building and its two-story tower. Sidewich Maldwort was behind the counter. When shown the pin, he recognized it immediately. It was his daughter Sadie's. He called her in from the back and gave her a short, pointed lecture on the subject of playing by the Stones. The Man of Gold kept a peculiar inventory: amber nectar that healed wounds and occasionally sent the drinker into a dead sleep, two hundred pieces a vial; devil's grease, a psychedelic fungal slime mold with an ultraviolet aura that smelled improbably of strange sausage and made the body feel vast and strange and close. Common Dolmenwood herbs were also on hand. Before they left, Maldwort mentioned that he had known Farnham Ribblemead — as a linguist, no less. He noted that spirithame could be found a day's walk northwest of Lankshorn, and pointed them toward Hay's Addle, a twenty-mile stretch along Lake Longmere where useful herbs like Marshwick could be gathered if one had the sense to avoid the hag. Blood canker grew near the Mossling domain, he added: good for a measure of healing, though not without risk to one's constitution.

Then Jack drew Sadie aside and asked about the Stones. The cool kids hung out there, she said. She'd seen an elf playing music once. Short creatures had appeared from thin air and spoken to him. Then they'd all vanished together. Jack and Henrick exchanged a look. They knew what had become of Lucien.

Gwomotom, meanwhile, was dining on rice and mash in the constabulary basement. His cell was small and damp, its narrow window giving onto the street at pavement level. Through it, over the following days, he made the acquaintance of a stray dog — small, curly-haired, floppy-eared, and possessed of a certain dignity. By the time they were done with each other, the dog had decided he belonged to Gwomotom.

That night, Jeremy found his way back to Hornstoat's Rest. The Constable had left the constabulary a few hours earlier and walked down the Ditchway into the woods. Whether toward crookhorn territory or somewhere else, the boy couldn't say.

The twenty-sixth came in cold and pouring. Tomkin, drifting back toward Lankshorn from the margins of his informal exile, made his way to a bladesmith he'd been hearing about. Joromy Whilptson-Puddingfoot was a compact man in his mid-forties, wide-set eyes sharp as his trade, hair slicked flat and black. He had no objection to Tomkin's allegiance to Lord Ramius. Tomkin traded his mace and shield for a longsword, and came away with something extra besides: Whilptson-Puddingfoot mentioned, in passing, that he practiced moon forging — the old art of tempering a blade under the full moon to draw in something that ordinary fire could not.

By the twenty-eighth the rain had cleared. Gwomotom was released from the constabulary. He named the dog Tom.

The twenty-ninth was warm and clear. At nine o'clock, while the rest of the party played I Spy in the common room of Hornstoat's Rest, Lucien walked in from the direction of the woods, flowers blooming briefly wherever his boots met the earth. He had spent five days with small blue goblins in the court of Princess Andromethia, and was somewhat the worse for fairy absinthe. He had also come away with a dragonfly hairpin, a gift or a keepsake of the stay.

He told them what he'd learned. Andromethia had confirmed the geography hidden inside Thinwhistle's riddle: the Naom waterfall fed into Lake Longmere, and a frost elf embassy stood nearby. She had pressed an invitation on him to return to the Blossom Fields whenever he wished, and left him with a warning. Andromethia had a sister. This sister ruled the Deathglades from a palace of white marble, under the title Lady of Spring Unending. The way Andromethia had said it was not casual. Lucien had also mapped, as best he could, the fairy roads threading through Dolmenwood. The Moggle Door near Lankshorn was the entrance Thinwhistle had hinted at — the road running north from there to Willowgate. A second fairy road, Buttercup Lane, connected the Rosy Gate in the northeast to the Emerald Door in the northwest.

The party, whole for the first time in days, turned to the matter of the Constable.

Jeremy led them out along the Ditchway to where the Constable had gone — a small clearing not far outside town. They sent the boy back to Lankshorn before anything interesting happened and cast around for signs. The clearing revealed nothing at first. Gwomotom drew a crow down with food and put the creature to some use. The crow had not seen anything of note recently. Small grey creatures frequented the area, though — things that simply vanished when one looked at them. Longer ago, it had observed a human in red armor standing in that spot, and heard a phrase spoken aloud there: Leeleeglablea. Jack and Henrick both knew the rumor of it: a crone summoned on moonlit nights by those syllables to purchase unwanted children.

That same day, Mabble came to Brother Henrick with something he'd witnessed. He had seen Father Dobey admit two male breggles to the church one night, late. Neither had returned since. The party recognized a lever when they held one.

Henrick went to Constable Wolder and put it to him plainly: something suspicious was afoot at the Church of St. Pastery, and they needed the Constable's authority to act on it. Wolder agreed to accompany him — as any guilty man tries to seem cooperative — and brought two guards along: Merrick and Esther. The rest of the party had already taken positions in the trees on either side of the path, a rope stretched taut between them at shin height. Tomkin threw his grappling hook at the Constable and missed. The rope went taut at the same moment, and Merrick went down hard. The Constable reached for Henrick and caught nothing. Henrick raised his hand and called upon God, and the divine hand fell upon Constable Wolder like a weight — the man stood rigid, every muscle locked, eyes burning with a fury that had nowhere to go. Jack knocked Merrick senseless with the pommel of his dagger. Tomkin brought the flat of his new blade across Esther's back, staggering her without dropping her. Gwomotom bound the paralyzed Constable with rope. Lucien secured Merrick. Esther ran. Tomkin went after her, struck her again, flat of the blade, and she went down. He tied her where she fell. Gwomotom considered the neatly packaged Constable with an expression of profound personal satisfaction. "What a nicely wrapped gift," he said, and pinched the man's cheek.

Jack searched Wolder's person. A crystal vial of liquid with a faint nutty aroma went into his pocket. Then Jack said the name "Shub's Nana" aloud, and watched the Constable's face do something involuntary.

Henrick brought the matter of Wolder's soul into the conversation, and the Constable was persuaded to talk.

He had wanted children to go missing from Lankshorn — not on direct orders, but out of something smaller and more personal. He wanted to make Lord Malbleat look incompetent, to clear the way for Lord Ramius to be reinstated. To that end he had found Shub's Nana: not a crookhorn, but a crone who served the Nag-Lord in her own right. She was sending the children north to be eaten. Silver goblins served as the mechanism. Wolder brought children to the hay field outside town where the goblins waited. The syllables of Leeleeglablea were how those goblins were summoned. The crone traveled by sleigh. The three breggles who had accosted Llerg at the Bogways — Marialla, James, and Fisk — were the ones who delivered children into goblin hands. The Constable himself had once been seized by the goblins near the Jaunty Horn inn along the Ditchway, transported nearly a day south to the crone's cottage — brick, set beside a dark and rushing brook — and returned to the woods outside Lankshorn before he'd had time to understand what had happened. That had been several months ago.

The party made their plan. The Constable would be sent back to Lankshorn. The children, and the woman responsible for them, were somewhere to the south...
