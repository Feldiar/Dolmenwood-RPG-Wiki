Session 3 — The Prisoner

Year 873 · Simswald · Days 24–25 Chilly and damp

The frozen pool and the chalice resting beneath the ice would have to wait. Dark passageways and the ever-present pipe music beckoned. The party stood at the four-way intersection beneath Droomen Knoll, the grimalkins arrayed to either side and Brother Henrick holding the lantern aloft. Ahead, the stench of guano announced the presence of beasts. The party took a left instead.

They were greeted by a stone sundial rising from the floor, its surface glazed with frost. An inscription had been cut into the face. The language was not one any of them could name, yet the words arrived whole and legible in their minds. On the slow creep of aeons, Mallow-heart's mercy may shine. Behind the sundial a bas-relief had been carved into the wall: a bitter-faced elf dressed in heavy furs, crowned with thorns set with gems. Lucien knew the face. Prince Mallowheart, estranged brother of the Cold Prince. Jack took the lantern and moved it closer to study the dial's markings. While the group talked through what they knew, the subject turned to the Drune, the sorcerers who had long ago allied with the Pluritine Church and the nobility to hold the Cold Prince and his frost elves at bay. Those old alliances had faltered, and now the Drune are known to many as the heretical "Hooded Men." The sundial's inscription, with its invocation of Mallowheart's mercy, remained suggestive without becoming clear. For now, the party returned to the intersection and turned toward the smell.

Streaks of sunlight pierced through the gaps in the rubble ahead. What clung to the ceiling did not improve on the smell. Five stirges, wings folded tight against their bodies, hung from the stone and stirred in the lantern light. Gwomotom put an arrow through one before the rest knew the fight had started. Then it was chaos.

The party was buffeted by the flapping of the winged creatures. Two dove for the grimalkins, driving their proboscises into fur and flesh, feasting on their victims' blood. Brother Henrick called on Saint Lillibeth to close Jack's wounds while Brennan skewered the stirge attached to the party's resident thief. To Brennan's dismay, it held on. Lucien dropped one with two volleys of his sling in quick succession. Tomkin smashed his mace into the stirge draining his kinsman as his own blood continued to be siphoned. Despite everything the party threw at it, the stirge remained buried in Tomkin's neck, draining the life from him by the second. He was at death's door before Lucien's sling finally ended it, with nothing at all to spare. Jack and Gwomotom made quick work of the remaining stirge. Tomkin sat down heavily and lit up some catnip. "Tomkin are you alright?" Jack said in Mewl. "I've been better." Gwomotom crouched over one of the dead stirges and pried loose a proboscis. Yet another trophy for his growing collection.

With the room quiet, the question of the puncture wounds on the breggle corpses answered itself. The stirges had been feasting.

Lucien began pulling at the rubble in the corner and found a hand. The attached body belonged to a human man in a black cloak, embroidered and dusty, the stitchwork carrying the markings of the Drune. He had been there for some time. Jack searched him and turned out a golden owl torque, a scroll seemingly arcane in nature, a platinum ring set with a topaz cut in the shape of an eye, and a smooth orange crystal roughly an inch across. When Lucien touched the crystal, his eyes rolled back. He was elsewhere: a stone altar sheeted in slime, a stag skeleton at its center, standing stones pressing close on every side, everything dripping with moss. Another image: a tangled forest, and among the branches, owls wrought from metal. A voice spoke from the vision. "Ill omens at the Whything Stones. Winter cometh." Then he was back, blinking at his own hands.

A door at the far end of the room had been smashed from its hinges and driven into the adjacent chamber. Frosted branches were carved into its broken panels. Tomkin, still moving despite everything, peered through.

Six large murals occupied recesses along the walls of what had once been a feasting hall. The figures were rendered in the same style as the frost elf frescoes encountered earlier in the underground ruins, and below each mural a name had been inscribed. As with the sundial, meaning was somehow derived. Prince Mallowheart was easily recognized, though his mural named him Prince Seven-Past-Noon. After him came Sir Dawn-Neverblooms, Lady Silver-So-Mirrored with her harp, the portly Lord Slumberbane, Whisper-Of-Candlelight, and at the far end, Famine-Whistles-Whither: depicted with a silver flute raised to his lips. Three blood-red hounds had been added to this mural.

They returned to the sundial. Jack held the lantern. Lucien held Jack. The inscription had given them the angle they needed: seven minutes past noon. They adjusted the beam, and the frost on the sundial's face began to melt. A breath of wind moved through the passage from somewhere behind them, carrying the pipe music with it. Brennan stepped to the front and led the party down the final unexplored passage while Lucien performed a counter melody, anticipating ill effects from the pipe music.

Two cells waited in the dark, stone walls reinforced with iron bars. The first was empty, the second was not. A frail figure crouched against the far wall, playing off-white pan pipes. Its black eyes flickered in the party's lantern light. Then the figure stood and began pacing the cell in erratic circuits, smiling between breaths, a shock of white hair catching the glow. He looked, very nearly, like the last mural in the feasting hall. It was Famine-Whistles-Whither. Tomkin threw out a greeting. The pipes went quiet.

"Have you come for me?" the figure said before resuming his playing. He called himself Thinwhistle. After offering food, the party came to learn what had brought him here: he had killed and eaten hounds belonging to Prince Mallowheart himself, and Mallowheart had locked him away for it. He soon proposed a bargain. Free him, and he would tell them what he knew about a fairy door capable of moving travelers great distances through Dolmenwood in a matter of steps. Beyond that, he had wants of his own: he intended, eventually, to see the barrier to Frigia reopened. The homeland of the frost elves had been sealed away long ago by the Drune with the Ring of Chel. For those who wished to cross, he said, there was a way. "Where the waters of Naon fall from great heights, where the watchers wait. You may be able to cross." He mentioned the wood gods who had dwelled in Dolmenwood before the frost elves and been imprisoned by the Drune out of jealousy for their power. The party asked about the chalice. An heirloom of Mallowheart, Thinwhistle said. In the empty cell beside his, Lucien spied a message scratched into the stone: Mallowheart be cursed; hound baker Thinwhistle be doubly cursed.

Thinwhistle urged the party to discover the mechanism to free him, not knowing that they had already inadvertently done so. The party backed away to confer. First, the chalice. Lucien and Henrick stayed at the intersection, tracking the pipe music to ensure Thinwhistle remained put. Gwomotom, Brennan, and the grimalkins slipped back to the icy pool. They recalled Lucien's old story of mortal blood and fairy ice. Brennan's blood was the key: drawn and pressed against the ice, his blood thawed the frozen pool. Tomkin threw his grappling hook into the pool and fished the chalice out. Within the vessel was a mere sip of metallic liquid, changing colors and refusing to spill regardless of how the cup was angled. To Tomkin, it smelled exactly like nippers. Gwomotom tasted a drop and felt a brief surge of energy move through his veins. Tomkin drank the rest. Something shifted in him. He felt faster, leaner, ready for anything.

He ran back down to the cell in a state of intoxication, feigned working some magic on the lock, and told Thinwhistle to try the door. Thinwhistle, elated when it opened, bolted for the dungeon exit, but the party caught him just long enough to collect what they had been promised. "Catch the edge of the wood to the west," he said. "Find a clearing of rat skulls and you will be whisked away. Entrances to fairy kingdoms appear on either side of the road for those with invitations. Stick to the road, and you will end up somewhere else in Dolmenwood." Before he went, he left a final message in the minds of the party:

"Where Naon's waters veil the stone, a starving court still dines alone. Nine hundred years on sugared lies, when last they drink, the last one dies. I cannot go. The watchers wait, root and horn beside the gate. But you may pass where I am banned, warm-breathed ghosts in fey-touched land."

Then he was gone. It was clear Emelda and Farnham Ribblemead were not here, so the party turned back toward Lankshorn. Somewhere along the way, the effects of the chalice on Tomkin subsided. Laden with treasure, the party returned to Lankshorn and made short work of spending it. The grimalkins celebrated as grimalkins do. Jack talked Tomkin into relieving himself against the Church of St. Pastery, and Father Dobey and Mabble came out and caught him in the act. Tomkin was run out of Lankshorn before the evening was properly over. Jack would have left with him out of solidarity if he hadn't first been whisked away by a company of fairies, who swept him into a revel at the court of Diuthurnia under Duke Maifluer. Things went well enough until Jack insulted the Duke's pride by attempting to ride his prized hunting trophy — a stuffed boar — at which point his welcome expired sharply. He returned in the morning knowing that the Duke kept hunting grounds in the Table Downs to the north.

Lucien also found himself transported. He had been taken to the Blossom Fields, the domain of Princess Andromethia, whose court was populated by creatures half-elf and half-satyr. Her lands of hills and meadows instilled a feeling close to ecstasy. Lucien, back in Fairy for the first time in a long while, found it easy to stay. Too easy. The party woke the next morning to find his bed empty, and the morning after, and the morning after that. He was gone for five days. Princess Andromethia, they would eventually learn, held lands in the mortal realm as well, somewhere in the south of Dolmenwood. Gwomotom somehow caught the ire of the town, landing him in the constabulary. Brother Henrick, meanwhile, enjoyed an evening of charity, distributing his transient wealth among the poor and hungry of Lankshorn, and tithing the Church of St. Pastery.

So it was that Jack and Henrick woke on the twenty-fifth of Simswald to find themselves a band of two. Of their five companions, one had been expelled from the town, one was incarcerated, and one had disappeared into Fairy.

Then Llerg appeared across the inn's common room, caught their eye, and drew them into a quiet corner. He had come for more bribe money. It turned out he had been approached at the Bogways by a group of breggles named Marialla, James, and Fisk, who frequented the place and made no secret of their admiration for Lord Shadgore Ramius and loathing of Lord Malbleat. These unsavory three were almost certainly affiliated with the crookhorn conspiracy and the constable. They had roughed him up in an attempt to gather information about the party. Jack and Henrick paid Llerg to put distance between himself and the trouble: south, toward High Hankle, for a few weeks. Llerg lamented he would miss the Hlerribuck breggle festival on the fourth of Parchment, eight days hence. Nevertheless, he pocketed the coin and departed. On the road south, he mentioned in passing, there was a shrine to Pusskin. Milk was the correct offering for those hoping for the old cat's favor.

The party was now divided, impoverished and pursued by agents of the Nag-Lord. Their prospects for the future had taken a turn for the worse...
