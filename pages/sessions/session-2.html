Session 2 — Below the Knoll

Year 873 · Simswald · Days 23–24 Gently drizzling (Day 24)

With little time to catch their breath after their skirmish with the crookhorn occupants of Droomen Knoll, the party descended from the courtyard into the dark unknown. From somewhere below came the sound of quiet breathing — perhaps the crookhorn that had fled unscathed? Jack lit a torch. Worn flagstones, damp stone, the ceiling close above them.

The torchlight washed over what lurked in the first room. Tied to the far wall by a rope: a two-headed beast, vaguely chicken-like, with a purple wattle and red-and-black feathers. One head was sleeping; the other noticed the party and screeched in agitation. Lucien, despite his bardic expertise in monsters, had never seen anything like it and suspected mutation. What would become known as the mutant chicken lunged toward the new intruders, but its beaks fell short as the rope pulled taut. Determined to claim a new pair of trophies, Gwomotom moved to engage. Jack slunk into a melee with his dagger and caught bites from both beaks, bringing him to the edge of death. Henrick's sling went wide, striking Gwomotom instead. Jack drew monster blood in retaliation and Gwomotom finished the job with two clean beheadings in a single motion. Henrick appealed to Saint Lillibeth, mending Jack's near-mortal wounds. With Henrick's holy power depleted, Gwomotom's injuries would have to wait for another day. Gwomotom set to work harvesting the corpse.

Lucien listened at the door now freed from its guardian and heard hushed whispers — surely this was their escaped crookhorn. While Gwomotom worked, Henrick studied the faded frescoes decorating the walls of the first room: white humanoid silhouettes standing in an icy forest. Jack and Tomkin, meanwhile, scouted down a hallway branching from the room and found a four-way intersection.

Doubling back to the door, Tomkin approached, shield in hand, with Gwomotom behind him, arrow nocked. They cracked open the door: a ring of candles, nasty bedding, crates — no crookhorns visible. Orange smoke from the candles flooded every nose in the party, thick and foul. On a silent countdown from three, Tomkin threw the door wide. No attack came. A body lay on the floor. Invoking his fairy glamour, Tomkin taunted the room in Gaff, the breggle tongue. From behind a stack of crates the escaped crookhorn emerged. "If you want me, come and get me," it bleated, foamy spittle spraying from its mouth. It shoved the crates in front of the door, and a second crookhorn emerged. Jack passed the torch to Henrick and leapt into action.

The fight was scrappy. Lucien's sling clipped one crookhorn; Gwomotom's arrow found the other. Only one horn adorned the head of the second crookhorn; the other was broken off. Spotting Henrick attempting to reach the body, it rushed the friar with a hefty club but missed — a miracle from God in Henrick's eyes. "Guard the prisoner!" the one-horned goatfolk bellowed. Tomkin held the line with grim patience, taking blows and giving them back. Jack tried slipping around the flank, got spotted, and improvised. Gwomotom's next arrow sank into the one-horned crookhorn, dealing the final blow. Lucien and Jack finished the rabid crookhorn from a distance. Jack, never missing an opportunity for monetary procurement, dredged up silver pieces from the bodies.

Tomkin searched the crates, finding not-so-edible food. A chest sat in the corner. The prisoner was unconscious but breathing — middle-aged, monk's haircut. It could only be Mabble, Father Dobey's assistant. After enduring the headaches from the pungent smoke, the party resolved to drag both the chest and Mabble back to the first room, where fresh air was more plentiful. Lucien examined the frescoes and identified the iconography: frost elves, which suggested some connection to the Cold Prince. Jack got to work inspecting the chest, discovered a needle in the lock, and disabled the trap mechanism. Inside the chest were heavy sacks of coin, two gold bands set with amber, and a scrap of paper crudely scrawled in Gaff. None of the party could read it.

The party woke Mabble and questioned him once they alleviated his initial panic upon waking. He had followed the sound of pipe music, been enchanted by the dulcet tones, and been struck from behind. He had not seen or heard any other prisoners in his fluctuating state of consciousness. The party decided to return to Lankshorn to rest, bank the treasure, and escort Mabble to safety.

Returning to town by early evening, they brought Mabble to Father Dobey's thatched-roof cottage. Dobey was pleased to see his assistant — though he perhaps exaggerated his elation — but was troubled to learn crookhorns were nesting so close. He dressed Mabble's head wound and ushered him to rest as the party departed.

From there, the party crossed to the constabulary, a building of stone and wood. A breggle clerk in red leather armor and a horned helmet met them at the front desk. They remained skeptical that crookhorns could be this close to town despite the party's claims. Finding no help from the clerk, the party insisted upon speaking to the constable. They were led to a back room where they met Constable Sidney Wolder, a human male. He refilled his drink as he made his greetings, offering none to the party until Lucien's passive-aggressive remarks prompted him to correct this oversight. After hearing the party's account of their excursion, Wolder was no more concerned than the clerk. He advised them not to return to Droomen Knoll, paid out the crookhorn bounty, and suggested that if they truly needed support, they ought to speak with Sgt. Marge Bregson. The party had come looking for official backing. They left without it.

Back at Hornstoat's Rest, the party found Llerg the breggle hunter eating pickled eggs at the bar. They asked him to read the note in Gaff. He went quiet. His face was a mask of concern as he relayed the contents of the message. It was a set of instructions — directing someone to contact Constable Sidney Wolder, apparently an agent of the Nag-Lord, creator of the crookhorns. It was all but assured that the constable was a spy in league with the crookhorns — and the party just gave him a detailed account of their assault on his conspirators. They bribed Llerg with gold for his silence, and Lucien played his mandolin in an attempt to charm him; it didn't take. Llerg kept the gold.

The party withdrew to their room to talk it over. What would they do next? Perhaps Sgt. Bregson could help? Or maybe she, too, was a spy? The party decided to test the waters in the morning. Until then, it was time to catalog the treasure. The coin was easy enough to divide, and the bracelets were bound to fetch a good price at Moot, the upcoming market day in Lankshorn.

That evening, the party bought rounds for the inn's regulars, angling for any adventurous soul willing to join an expedition into the Knoll. The conversation was warm; the takers were not. Not even the grimalkins had luck at the rough-and-tumble Bogways. The party was still without aid. Lucien took the stage at Hornstoat's Rest and played the traditional elven ballads Don't Look Back in Fury and Wonder Well to warm applause and a comped tab. Jack paid one of Margery the innkeeper's sons to carry a letter to the constabulary — a false itinerary, claiming the party would depart on Moot, a day later than they actually planned. If Wolder meant to arrange anything unpleasant on the road, this gave him the wrong window.

What remained uncertain was Sgt. Bregson. The next morning — Hayme, the 24th of Simswald, the day before Moot — the party arranged a meeting, yielding no definitive proof of her allegiance but a helping hand as consolation. The sergeant showed no more enthusiasm for the crookhorn problem than the constable, but after a period of conversational attrition, she assigned Brennan, one of her recruits, to accompany them on their return to Droomen Knoll. Brennan was a fresh-faced lad with a bright smile, unmarred leather armor, and a polished shield and spear. He was hardly a combat veteran, but he was better than nothing. Bregson specified that Brennan was serving as an informant for the town guard and would not be permitted to serve as a shield of the human variety.

They set out on the Ditchway again through a gentle drizzle. At the ruins, Jack ranged ahead along the treeline to scout. The courtyard was empty; the pipe music still played. The party once again descended into the bowels of Droomen Knoll, this time accompanied by Brennan.

Something was different. Tomkin spotted it first. Small puncture wounds in the breggle corpses, made sometime after the party had left. Careful, deliberate wounds. The postulation was grim: someone had made sure no breggle survived to tell what it had seen.

In the storage room, Jack investigated the high stack of crates and found a crack in the wall behind them — a hollow place that no one had noticed the day before. A hidden button. A click. He pushed, and a section of wall swung open. A gust of snow and icy wind rushed through the opening. Beyond lay a corridor beset by a snowstorm. The pipe music still drifted from beyond the four-way intersection, not from here.

Beyond the hallway was a room just as thick with rime. A raised stone pool dominated the space — frozen solid, stag skull carvings decorating the basin, glittering lights shimmering beneath the ice. Snow fell steadily from the ceiling. The walls bore more long-faded frescoes. Lucien had heard a legend of exactly this: a pool and the chalice of great beauty said to rest within it. The old tales spoke of fairy ice and mortal blood. There it was beneath the ice, exactly as the legend described. A gloriously wrought chalice, sparkling in the frozen pool. An air bubble seemed caught inside it. The party looked at it for a long moment. Though caught by the allure of the chalice, they resealed the chamber and pressed on down the unexplored passages, the pipe music still echoing through the dark...
