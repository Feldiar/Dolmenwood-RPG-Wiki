Session 5 — The Judgement of the Unicorn

Year 873 · Simswald 29 – Parchment 1 Muggy on Simswald 29, warm and sunny on Parchment 1

The Constable, lashed tight and somewhat chastened, was at least cooperative when it came time to negotiate terms. The guards Merrick and Esther, loyal to the man rather than to any broader conspiracy, were cut loose and sent back to Lankshorn. Wolder himself was given clearer instructions: return to Lankshorn, act as though nothing had changed, and continue reaching out to Shub's Nana as he normally would. He would lead the party south along the Ditchway toward the Jaunty Horn first, and after that, he would point them at the crone's cottage before taking his leave.

They set out along the Ditchway at a pace the Constable seemed happy enough to match. Gwomotom helped himself to a fistful of nightworm spawn — pale, wriggling, and not unpleasant if one had the stomach for it — and chewed thoughtfully as the landscape shifted. The sandy birchwood of Lankshorn's outskirts gave way to older forest, its trees thick and creaking in the wind, birdsong replaced by the low groan of wood under strain. The soil here was orange, as though blood had long ago been mixed into the earth and never left. Dense underbrush pressed in on either side, the kind that would tear at a person trying to move through it off-road.

Gwomotom coaxed a hawk down from the canopy with an offering of spawn. The bird was willing enough to negotiate. It called itself "I am speed," and reported that it mostly saw big adult nightworms when it looked down. It knew, however, that the adult worms congregated to the north, near large rocks and holes in the earth. It had young to feed. Gwomotom thanked it and let it go.

The Jaunty Horn appeared roughly eight and a half miles from Lankshorn, a half-day's walk along the Ditchway as it cut deeper into the earth, the road sunk below the forest floor with walls of soil rising on either side. The inn was lively. Two children — siblings, by the look of them — careened around the common room. Hundreds of horseshoes covered the walls. The proprietor was a woman named Jezebel, stout and muscular, late thirties, dressed in the garb of an adventurer. She had bought this place with adventuring loot and seemed content with the trade, mostly. She turned up an old bottle of nippers in her stock, and the two grimalkins of the party were glad of it.

The party settled in. Tomkin lit his pipeleaf. A merchant sat alone at a table in the common room, eating without apparent enthusiasm. Another group of travelers occupied a second table. Gwomotom, surveying the menu, found himself thinking with some longing of Glubwob — the mossling delicacy back home, a grayish slop of processed lichen that induced a deeply cathartic vomiting. The Jaunty Horn's menu did not offer anything comparable. They ordered what they could get.

Jezebel's eyes found Constable Wolder and did something he clearly wished they hadn't. He became uncomfortable in the way of a man reminded of his own humiliation. She had recognized him, and he had placed himself in memory once again in the hands of silver goblins near this very inn — seized during a previous visit and transported south in a manner he had never fully accounted for. Jezebel had heard tell of a yellow bile wyrm south of Prigwort, chased off by a knight before anyone could settle things properly. She'd never seen the beast herself, but she dreamed of it — of finding a wyrm and going at it as her last great hurrah. The inn had cost her most of what she'd earned adventuring, and the itch had not left.

While they sat with their food and pipes, a sound drifted in from the road outside — the kind of commotion that suggests a crowd carrying something heavy, and not happily. Jack went to the door. Coming along the Ditchway from the east, from the direction of Dreg, was a company of adventurers: seven of them, bearing an eighth between them, a female breggle rendered insensible and limp, grey-furred, dressed in grey clothes, her eyes when glimpsed showing milky and white. Her name, they learned, was Brehder. Among the traveling party walked a female breggle named Berrild, one of whose eyes had been replaced entirely by a yellow crystal, and a male breggle named Crump. They identified themselves as the Wooden Wolves — clearly an adventuring party in their own right — and wore the green and yellow of Lord Malbleat's colors. They had come to ease Brehder's suffering, and explained what had befallen her: she had touched a strange stone somewhere in the wilderness, and the color and vitality had drained out of her entirely. The land around the stone had been drained as well, the wilderness nearby left grey and sapped of life.

Henrick cast Detect Magic over the assembled company and found the air thick with it. Brehder herself glowed. Berrild's crystal eye glowed. Somewhere among the group, a ring held enchantment, and armor on a male breggle, and a pair of purple boots. Whatever the Wooden Wolves were carrying, they were well-equipped for something.

Gwomotom slipped out to hunt but found nothing. Tomkin, keeping one eye on the male breggle in the magic armor, eventually tried to do something about it — shifting to cat form in the dark, climbing the inn's exterior toward the window where the breggle slept. He slipped. The ground was not far, but it was unforgiving.

Jack turned his attention to the merchant eating alone, who proved to be a man named Fostis Mirk. The smell of fishmonger clung to him. Six generations of his family had worked the fish trade, all from Dreg, and he himself was traveling to Lankshorn for the breggle festival. He sold fish bullion, which apparently had a market. He had thoughts about Dreg, most of them concerning a zealot there named Hogbeard — a holy man who could not be trusted, who wore yellow smocks along with his followers, who believed the town might improve considerably if a select few people were removed from it, and who was not shy about forcing sinners to their knees in public repentance. Dreg was famed for its bogwine sausages. Shantywood Isle was a haunt of miscreants and shady figures who preferred not to be found.

Before the evening was done, Henrick found a quiet moment with the Constable and put the question of Father Dobey to him plainly: what did he know about why breggles had been visiting the church at night? Wolder said he didn't know. Watching his face, Henrick believed him — which raised its own questions about what Father Dobey was doing that the Constable himself hadn't been brought in on.

The morning of the first of Parchment came in warm and sunny. Constable Wolder, having delivered what he could of value, pointed the party toward where he believed Shub's Nana's cottage lay and turned back for Lankshorn. They were on their own again.

Henrick foraged and came back with five rations of fairy veil — a strange substance, silver-latticed like spun glass, smelling of leeks, tasting improbably of kiwi. Gwomotom went hunting and found four boars moving through the underbrush a hundred and twenty feet off. The party took to the trees in preparation for an ambush. Gwomotom's first arrow found its mark, sinking into a boar's flank, and the animals bolted in a panic. A blood trail led into the undergrowth. They followed it for a while before losing it entirely. No meat. They moved on.

The forest opened here and there into stands of tall silver birches, with criss-crossing paths between the trunks that suggested something — animals, travelers, or something else — moved through with regularity. In a glade not far along, the party found a circle of scarlet toadstools, each two feet high, ringing a sandstone slab ten feet tall with a crack running straight down the center. Markings covered its surface in a script none of them could read or name. It looked old. Jack lobbed a stone into the toadstool ring as an experiment. Nothing happened. The party approached to study the markings more closely.

The toadstools burst their spores without warning. The coughing started, and then the world began to shift. Hallucinations moved through the party in waves of Pluritine iconography — saints beckoning from the corners of vision, sacred figures reaching across some threshold the spores had temporarily dissolved. The marks on the stone seemed to rearrange themselves in Jack's perception, arcane at one moment and then — in a way that made perfect sense to someone breathing hallucinogenic spores — clearly a shrine to the rats, a declaration that the rats were coming to take over the world. The argument that followed had a dream logic to it: Gwomotom, seized by ambition, declared he would dig an underground domain and rule it as his own. Jack, equally convinced that the rats lived below and that any digging would release them upon the world, was firmly and urgently opposed. The argument escalated. Gwomotom launched Jack bodily into a mushroom, which resolved nothing.

The figure at the edge of the glade was hard to miss once you noticed him: six and a half feet tall, a human man with a shaved head, a brass nose ring, a dark cloak, and a curved dagger sheathed at his side. Small figures made of sticks accompanied him in the manner of a retinue. He wore a bracelet of carved bone, the carvings taking the shape of mushrooms. He was Drune.

"This area is not for mortals or fairies," he said. "This is the domain of the Drune alone. You are to leave at once and never return."

He gave his name as Aetho Grym. He called this place the Golokstone. The party backed away and did not press the matter further.

It was nighttime by the time they made camp. Henrick turned the fairy veil into a stew. Tomkin, in an expansive mood, told a story about Moggle Whipple-tongue, the grimalkin explorer of some renown — or at least renown within certain circles. Henrick did not believe a word of it but was entertained anyway.

In the depths of the night, while keeping watch, Tomkin saw something step out of the trees: a horse, pure white, its coat shimmering faintly in the dark. It continued on through the woods. A man stepped from the darkness and told Tomkin, without preamble, to stay out of it, then disappeared back into the trees in the direction the horse had gone.

Tomkin followed anyway. He found them a hundred feet from camp — the man, who was furious in the way of someone who had been waiting a long time for something, and the horse, which was not, on closer inspection, a horse at all. The creature had a long horn and a ten-foot tail and a rainbow aura that shimmered at its edges. The man said he had found it first. He said he needed the unicorn to lift a curse. The unicorn approached the man and pressed its horn to him. Tomkin used his fairy gift to understand what it said.

"You have been judged unworthy," the unicorn said, in a voice that was feminine and final. "Find another way to lift your curse."

The man wept. Tomkin waited until the unicorn was leaving before he called after it.

"You have made your judgement of this man," he said. "I ask that you make your judgement of me. I must know what kind of person I really am."

The unicorn paused. It seemed to find this interesting. When it spoke to him, it said: "It seems you are going down a darker path than you intended. Think on what you truly want." Then Tomkin's longsword began to glow with a quiet light, temporarily blessed by the unicorn.

The man was not pleased by this. He had fangs, it turned out, which he revealed in an attempt to use them on Tomkin. The flat of the blade fended him off. "You're not worth it," the man said. "I will try to improve my worth," said Tomkin, "by first forgiving you for that." The man went quiet. He said he wanted to be how he once was. Tomkin said they could only be what they are. He offered the man a place by the fire for the night.

The man said he had once been called Alfred. The rest of the party met him in the morning — dirty, wild-haired, bearded, somewhere in his mid-forties. Henrick looked at him plainly and offered what he had to give: the One True God might work through him to lift whatever affliction had taken hold, if Alfred would only ask. Alfred asked. Henrick laid his hands on him, and the lycanthropy left Alfred's body — the curse broken, the wolf put to rest. Alfred briefly lost consciousness. He came back to himself with bluer eyes and a face slightly younger, hairier but somehow cleaner, like something restored. He had come from Brackenwold, where the curse had been placed on him. Henrick turned over what he knew and formed a quiet suspicion about who at Brackenwold might have done it.

They gave Alfred the choice to go his own way or join them. He chose to stay, hoping for some form of redemption. The party decided to make a brief stop in Dreg to resupply and see Alfred properly equipped before resuming the hunt for the crone.

Before they broke camp, the party climbed to the top of a tall tree and took stock of the land. To the west lay Lankshorn. Droomen Knoll rose to the northwest. The Jaunty Horn stood to the north. To the northeast, a grey depression in the earth held a large crystal at its center, catching the light strangely. Dreg lay to the east. To the southeast, a large house stood with a barn beside it. Due south, an old cottage sat by a small river with meadows stretching beyond. To the southwest, the uneven humps of burial mounds broke the line of the earth. The land opened around them, full of things that had names they didn't yet know...
